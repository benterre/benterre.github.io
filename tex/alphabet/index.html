Symbols and Alphabets

Symbols and alphabets, those are cool aren't they?

Definitions and Properties

An alphabet \(\mathcal{A}\) is a set of functions in the some variables \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\). Such a function \(a\in\mathcal{A}\) is called a letter. A Multiple PolyLogarithm (or MPL or Goncharov function) \(F^{(w)}\) of weight \(w\) is a function of the variables \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) which obeys $$dF^{(w)} = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}} F^{(w-1)}_a d\ln(a)$$ where \(F^{(w-1)}_a\) are such functions of weight \(w-1\).
By the above property, we can derive a condition on the \(d\ln\) terms. This is known as the integrability condition $$d^2F^{(w)} = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}} dF^{(w-1)}_a\wedge d\ln(a) = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}}\sum_{b\in\mathcal{A}}F^{(w-2)}_{ab}d\ln(b)\wedge d\ln(a) = 0$$ To every MPL \(F^{(w)}\) we assign a symbol \(\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(w)}\right]\) as $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(w)}\right] = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}}\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(w-1)}_a\right]\otimes a = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}}\sum_{b\in\mathcal{A}}\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(w-2)}_{ab}\right]\otimes b\otimes a$$

The dilogarithm \(\text{Li}_2\)

For example, let us consider the dilogarithm \(\text{Li}_2(x) = \int_0^x\frac{dx^\prime}{x^\prime}\int_0^{x^\prime}\frac{dx^{\prime\prime}}{1-x^{\prime\prime}}\). One indeed sees that \(\text{Li}_2\) is a MPL as it obeys $$d\text{Li}_2(x) = \frac{dx}{x}\int_0^x\frac{dx^\prime}{1-x^\prime} = \int_0^x\frac{dx^\prime}{1-x^\prime}d\ln(x)$$ where, of course, we have $$d\int_0^x\frac{dx^\prime}{1-x^\prime} = \frac{dx}{1-x} = -d\ln(1-x)$$ We can thus compute the symbol of the dilogarithm $$\mathcal{S}\left[\text{Li}_2\right] = \mathcal{S}\left[\int_0^x\frac{dx^\prime}{1-x^\prime}\right]\otimes x = -(1-x)\otimes x$$

The polylogarithm \(\text{Li}_k\)

Let us now consider the polylogarithm of order \(k\in\mathbb{N}\) whose definition is $$\text{Li}_k(x) = \int_0^x\frac{dx_k}{x_k}\int_0^{x_k}\frac{dx_{k-1}}{x_{k-1}}\cdots \int_0^{x_3}\frac{dx_2}{x_2}\int_0^{x_2}\frac{dx_1}{1-x_1}$$ One can prove recursively that \(\text{Li}_k\) is a MPL as it obeys $$d\text{Li}_k(x) = \frac{dx}{x}\text{Li}_{k-1}(x) = \text{Li}_{k-1}(x)d\ln(x)$$ As before, we calculate the symbol of this function $$\mathcal{S}\left[\text{Li}_k\right] = \mathcal{S}\left[\text{Li}_{k-1}\right]\otimes x = \cdots = -(1-x)\otimes x\otimes \cdots\otimes x$$

An example: \(A_2\)

Let us have a look at the space of symbols generated by the alphabet \(\mathcal{A} = \{x, \,y, \,1+x, \,1+y, \,1+x+y\}\). This alphabet originates from the quiver \(A_2\).

For each weight \(w\) there are \(\lvert\mathcal{A}\rvert^w\) independent symbols. In other words, the space of all symbols of weight \(w\) has dimension \(\lvert\mathcal{A}\rvert^w\). However, not all of these symbols originate from MPL. Indeed, we need to impose the integrability condition in order to find the true space of symbols. Let us perform this for different weight values.

Weight \(w=0\)

This case is trivial as only one symbol is possible: that corresponding to constant functions. i.e. 0

Weight \(w=1\)

All weight one MPL obey the integrability condition by definition. Hence a general weight one MPL function \(F^{(1)}(x,y)\) has symbol $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(1)}(x,y)\right] = \sum_{i=1}^5 c^{(1)}_i a_i\qquad a_i\in\mathcal{A}$$ In other words, the space of symbols of weight one is spanned by the alphabet.

Weight \(w=2\)

A general weight 2 MPL function \(F^{(2)}(x,y)\) has symbol $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(2)}(x,y)\right] = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 c^{(2)}_{ij} a_i\otimes a_j\qquad a_i, a_j\in\mathcal{A}$$ where the constants \(c^{(2)}_{ij}\) will obey some conditions. Let's derive those conditions using the integrability condition.
We indeed have $$dF^{(2)}(x,y) = \sum_{i=1}^5 F^{(1)}(x,y)d\ln(a_i)$$ $$d^2F^{(2)}(x,y) = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 c^{(2)}_{ij}d\ln(a_i)\wedge d\ln(a_j) = 0$$ Since \(a_i(x,y)\) are functions of \(x,y\) we can expand the above into $$\begin{align}d^2F^{(2)}(x,y) & = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 \frac{c^{(2)}_{ij}}{a_ia_j}da_i\wedge da_j\\ & = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 \frac{c^{(2)}_{ij}}{a_ia_j}\left(\frac{\partial a_i}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial a_i}{\partial y}dy\right)\wedge \left(\frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial y}dy\right)\\ & = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 \frac{c^{(2)}_{ij}}{a_ia_j}\left(\frac{\partial a_i}{\partial x}\frac{\partial a_j}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x}\frac{\partial a_i}{\partial y}\right)dx\wedge dy\\ & = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5c^{(2)}_{ij}A_{ji}dx\wedge dy\\ & = \text{Tr}(C^{(2)}A(x,y))dx\wedge dy\\ & = 0 \end{align}$$ where we have defined the matrix elements $$A_{ij} = \frac{1}{a_ia_j}\left(\frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x}\frac{\partial a_i}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial a_i}{\partial x}\frac{\partial a_j}{\partial y}\right)$$ Using the alphabet provided earlier, we can compute the elements of the antisymmetric matrix \(A(x,y)\) $$A(x,y) = \frac{1}{xy}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 & -\frac{y}{1+y} & -\frac{y}{1+x+y}\\ 1 & 0 & \frac{x}{1+x} & 0 & \frac{x}{1+x+y}\\ 0 & -\frac{x}{1+x} & 0 & -\frac{xy}{(1+x)(1+y)} & -\frac{xy}{(1+x)(1+x+y)}\\ \frac{y}{1+y} & 0 & \frac{xy}{(1+x)(1+y)} & 0 & \frac{xy}{(1+y)(1+x+y)}\\ \frac{y}{1+x+y} & -\frac{x}{1+x+y} & \frac{xy}{(1+x)(1+x+y)} & -\frac{xy}{(1+y)(1+x+y)} & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$ If we separate the matrix \(C^{(2)}\) into its symmetric and antisymmetric part, we notice that the symmetric part automatically satisfies the integrability condition (since \(A(x,y)\) is antisymmetric). Thus, we will focus on the antisymmetric part of \(C^{(2)}\) from now on. If we write $$C^{(2)}_{\text{A}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & c_{12} & c_{13} & c_{14} & c_{15}\\ -c_{12} & 0 & c_{23} & c_{24} & c_{25}\\ -c_{13} & -c_{23} & 0 & c_{34} & c_{35}\\ -c_{14} & -c_{24} & -c_{34} & 0 & c_{45}\\ -c_{15} & -c_{25} & -c_{35} & -c_{45} & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$ then the integrability condition becomes $$\begin{align}\text{Tr}(C^{(2)}_{\text{A}}A(x,y)) & = \frac{2c_{12}}{xy} + \frac{2c_{14}}{x(1+y)} + \frac{2c_{15}}{x(1+x+y)} - \frac{2c_{23}}{(1+x)y} - \frac{2c_{25}}{(1+x+y)y}\\ & + \frac{2c_{34}}{(1+x)(1+y)} + \frac{2c_{35}}{(1+x)(1+x+y)} + \frac{2c_{45}}{(1+x+y)(1+y)}\\ & = 0 \end{align}$$ Putting all terms on the same denominator and grouping with respect to the variables \(x,y\) we find the multivariable polynomial equation $$\begin{align}0= & -2c_{12} + x(-4c_{12} + 2c_{23} + 2c_{25}) + y(-4c_{12} - 2c_{14} - 2c_{15}) + x^2(-2c_{12} + 2c_{23} + 2c_{25})\\ & + y^2(-2c_{12} - 2c_{14} - 2c_{15}) + xy(-6c_{12} - 4c_{14} -2c_{15} + 4c_{23} + 2c_{25} - 2c_{34} - 2c_{35} + 2c_{45})\\ & + x^2y(-2c_{12} - 2c_{14} + 2c_{23} + 2c_{25} - 2c_{34} + 2c_{45}) + xy^2(-2c_{12} - 2c_{14} - 2c_{15} + 2c_{23} - 2c_{34} -2c_{35}) \end{align}$$ Solving this system of equations gives us the constraints $$\begin{matrix} c_{12} = c_{34} = 0 & c_{23} = c_{35} = -c_{25} & c_{14} = c_{45} = -c_{15} \end{matrix}$$ From the original 10 independent matrix elements of \(C^{(2)}_{\text{A}}\) we are left with 4 independent ones (since \(c_{13}\) and \(c_{24}\) were not contrained by the trace). So with 15 independent symbols from the symmetric part of \(C^{(2)}\) and these additional 4, there are a total of 19 independent symbols which originate from MPL of weight 2. There are the symmetric ones $$\begin{align} & x\otimes x \qquad y\otimes y \qquad (1+x)\otimes(1+x) \qquad (1+y)\otimes(1+y) \qquad (1+x+y)\otimes(1+x+y)\\ & x\otimes y + y\otimes x \qquad x\otimes(1+x) + (1+x)\otimes x \qquad x\otimes(1+y) + (1+y)\otimes x\\ & x\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+x+y)\otimes x \qquad y\otimes(1+x) + (1+x)\otimes y\\ & y\otimes(1+y) + (1+y)\otimes y \qquad y\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+x+y)\otimes y\\ & (1+x)\otimes(1+y) + (1+y)\otimes(1+x) \qquad (1+x)\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+x+y)\otimes(1+x)\\ & (1+y)\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+x+y)\otimes(1+y) \end{align}$$ and the antisymmetric ones $$\begin{align} y\otimes(1+x) - y\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+x)\otimes(1+x+y) - (\text{left}\leftrightarrow \text{right}) \qquad y\otimes(1+y) - (1+y)\otimes y\\ x\otimes(1+y) - x\otimes(1+x+y) + (1+y)\otimes(1+x+y) - (\text{left}\leftrightarrow \text{right}) \qquad x\otimes(1+x) - (1+x)\otimes x \end{align}$$

Weight \(w=3\)

A general weight 3 MPL function \(F^{(3)}(x,y)\) has symbol $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(3)}(x,y)\right] = \sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5\sum_{k=1}^5 c^{(3)}_{ijk} a_i\otimes a_j\otimes a_k\qquad a_i, a_j, a_k\in\mathcal{A}$$ where the constants \(c^{(3)}_{ijk}\) will obey some conditions. To see this, let us note that the space of symbols of weight 2 can be extended to the space of symbols of weight 3 by adding to each basis symbol five different letters. Indeed, since the space of symbols of weight 2 has dimension 19, that of weight 3 will have at most dimension \(5\times 19 = 95\). Let us denote these weight 2 basis symbols as $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(2)}_\alpha\right] = \sum_{i=1}^{5}\sum_{j=1}^{5}c^{(2)}_{\alpha ij}a_i\otimes a_j\qquad \alpha\in\{1,\ldots,19\}$$ where \(F^{(2)}_\alpha\) is a MPL function of weight 2.

Weight \(w=k\)

Let us now prove that all symbols for this alphabet at any weight \(w=k\geq 2\) are given by tensors whose pair-wise adjacent contractions with \(A(x,y)\) vanish. In other words $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(k)}(x,y)\right]=\sum_{i_1,\ldots, i_k}c^{(k)}_{i_1\ldots i_k}a_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes a_{i_k}\qquad 1\leq \alpha \leq k-1,\,\sum_{i_{\alpha},i_{\alpha+1}}c^{(k)}_{i_1\ldots i_{\alpha}i_{\alpha+1}\ldots i_k} A_{i_{\alpha+1}i_{\alpha}}=0$$ We have already shown that is the case for \(w=2\) and \(w=1\). Let's prove by recurrence that this statement holds for all \(w=k\geq 2\).
Suppose the statement is true for some \(w=k\) and \(w=k-1\). Then, there exist \(F^{(k)}_{\alpha}\) and \(c^{(k)}_{i_1\ldots i_k}\) such that $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(k)}_{\alpha}(x,y)\right]=\sum_{i_1,\ldots, i_k}c^{(k)}_{\alpha\, i_1\ldots i_k}a_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes a_{i_k}$$ spans the space of symbols of weight \(w=k\) and where \(\alpha\) ranges from \(1\) to the dimension of that said space \(D^k\) (and similarly for weight \(w=k-1\)).
We can extend this basis to one of the space of symbols of weight \(w=k+1\) by addition of a letter, of which there are five possibilities. As a consequence, a general weight \(w=k+1\) MPL will have symbol $$\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(k+1)}(x,y)\right]=\sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{\alpha = 1}^{D^k}d_{\alpha i}\mathcal{S}\left[F^{(k)}_{\alpha}(x,y)\right]\otimes a_{i}$$ where $$c^{(k+1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k+1}} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{D^k} c^{(k)}_{\alpha\,i_1\cdots i_k}\,d_{\alpha i_k}$$ The integrability condition then reads $$\begin{align} d^2 F^{(k+1)}(x,y) & = d\left( \sum_{i=1}^5 \sum_{\alpha = 1}^{D^k}d_{\alpha i}F^{(k)}_{\alpha}(x,y) d\ln(a_i) \right)\\ & = \sum_{i=1}^5 \sum_{\alpha = 1}^{D^k}d_{\alpha i}\,dF^{(k)}_{\alpha}(x,y)\wedge d\ln(a_i)\\ & = \sum_{i,j}\sum_{i_1,\ldots,i_{k-1}}\sum_{\alpha = 1}^{D^k}d_{\alpha i}\,F^{(k-1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1}}c^{(k)}_{\alpha\, i_1\cdots i_{k-1} j}d\ln(a_j)\wedge d\ln(a_i)\\ & = \sum_{i,j}\sum_{i_1,\ldots,i_{k-1}}\sum_{\alpha = 1}^{D^k}d_{\alpha i}\,F^{(k-1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1}}c^{(k)}_{\alpha\, i_1\cdots i_{k-1} j}\, A_{ij}\,dx\wedge dy\\ & = \sum_{i,j}\sum_{i_1,\ldots,i_{k-1}}F^{(k-1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1}}c^{(k+1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1} j i}\, A_{ij}\,dx\wedge dy\\ & = 0 \end{align}$$ Since this must hold for all \(F^{(k-1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1}}\) we find the condition $$\forall i_1,\ldots,i_{k-1},\,\forall x,y,\quad\sum_{i,j}c^{(k+1)}_{i_1\cdots i_{k-1} j i}\, A_{ij}(x,y)=0$$ Thus proving the statement for all \(k\geq 2\).

Solving computationally

In order to find all independ solutions to the equation described in the previous section we can introduce a new notation. Let \(f^{(k-1)}_{\alpha}\) be a symbol of weight \(k-1\) such that \((f^{(k-1)}_{\alpha})_{1\leq \alpha\leq D^{k-1}}\) is a basis of the space of symbols of that weight. Then the space of weight \(k\) symbols has basis elements $$f^{(k)}_\alpha=\sum_{\beta=1}^{D^{k-1}}\sum_{i=1}^5 c^{(k)}_{\alpha\beta i}\, f^{(k-1)}_{\beta}\otimes a_i\qquad 1\leq\alpha\leq D^k$$ We can then find a similar expression for symbols of weight \(k+1\) $$\begin{align} f^{(k+1)}_\alpha & =\sum_{\beta=1}^{D^{k}}\sum_{i=1}^5 c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha\beta i} f^{(k)}_{\beta}\otimes a_i\\ & = \sum_{\beta=1}^{D^{k}}\sum_{\gamma=1}^{D^{k-1}}\sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha\beta i}c^{(k)}_{\beta\gamma j}\, f^{(k-1)}_{\gamma}\otimes a_j\otimes a_i \end{align}$$ and impose the integrability condition on the coefficients \(c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha\beta i}\) by means of the constraint matrices \(Y_m\). One can derive those matrices from the matrix \(A(x,y)\) defined earlier. The integrability condition thus reads $$\sum_{\beta=1}^{D^{k}}\sum_{i=1}^5\sum_{j=1}^5 c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha\beta i}c^{(k)}_{\beta\gamma j} Y_{m i j}=0$$ We can then define the combined indices \(I=(\beta i)\) and \(J=(m\gamma)\) (i.e. \(0\leq I\leq 5D^k\) and \(0\leq J\leq 6D^{k-1}\)) to write the above equation as $$\sum_{I=1}^{5D^k}c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha I}\mathcal{C}_{IJ}^{(k)}=0$$ where $$\mathcal{C}_{IJ}^{(k)}=\sum_{j=1}^5c^{(k)}_{\beta\gamma j}Y_{m i j}$$ We can find the \(D^{(k+1)}\) vectors \(c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha}\) that obey the integrability condition by finding the null space of the matrix \(\mathcal{C}^{(k)}\) and then remapping the coefficient \(c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha I}\rightarrow c^{(k+1)}_{\alpha\beta i}\).
One advantage of using the constraint matrices \(Y_m\) is due to the flexibility of the method which can be applied to alphabets of any number of variables.

This limit will then correspond to the \(\delta\rightarrow 0\) limit of the redefinitions given by arXiv:1809.05101 $$\begin{align} & v_1 = \frac{s_{12}s_{23}}{s_{12}\delta (1+\beta\delta) + s_{23}(1+\delta+\beta\delta^2)}\\ & v_2 = s_{23}\\ & v_3 = -\frac{s_{12}^2z}{s_{23}(-1+z)\beta\delta + s_{12}(-1+(-1+z)\beta\delta)}\\ & v_4 = \frac{s_{23}(s_{12}+s_{23})\beta\delta^2}{s_{12}\delta(1+\beta\delta) + s_{23}(1+\delta+\beta\delta^2)}\\ & v_5 = \frac{s_{12}s_{23}(-1+z)}{s_{23}(-1+z)\beta\delta + s_{12}(-1+(-1+z)\beta\delta)} \end{align}$$ In the corresponding symbols, any occurence of \(d\ln(c)\) will vanish but \(d\ln(\delta)\) will not as the logarithm diverges. When taking the colinear limit we can thus discard all constant letters except for vanishing ones.
For example, in the neighbourhood of \(\delta=0\) $$\sqrt{\Delta(v)} = (s_{12}+s_{23})(s_{12} z + s_{23}\beta(1-z))\delta + o(\delta)$$ Taking the limit we find $$\begin{array}{ll} & W_1 \rightarrow s_{12} & = X_1\\ & W_2 \rightarrow s_{23} & = X_2\\ & W_3 \rightarrow s_{12}z & = X_3\\ & W_4 \rightarrow (s_{12}+s_{23})\beta\delta^2 & = X_4\\ & W_5 \rightarrow -s_{23}(z-1) & = X_5\\ & W_6 \rightarrow s_{12}z = X_3\\ & W_7 \rightarrow s_{23}(z-1) = X_5\\ & W_8 \rightarrow s_{12} - s_{23}(z-1) & = X_6\\ & W_9 \rightarrow s_{12}+s_{23} & = X_7\\ & W_{10} \rightarrow s_{12}z + s_{23} & = X_8\\ & W_{11} \rightarrow s_{12} = X_1\\ & W_{12} \rightarrow s_{23}z=X_2+X_3-X_1\\ & W_{13} \rightarrow s_{12}(z-1)=X_1+X_5-X_2\\ & W_{14} \rightarrow -s_{23} = -X_2\\ & W_{15} \rightarrow -s_{12}z - s_{23}(z-1) & = X_{9}\\ & W_{16} \rightarrow s_{12}+s_{23} = X_7\\ & W_{17} \rightarrow (s_{12}+s_{23})z=X_7+X_3-X_1\\ & W_{18} \rightarrow s_{12}(z-1) = X_1+X_5-X_2\\ & W_{19} \rightarrow -s_{23}z = X_2+X_3-X_1\\ & W_{20} \rightarrow (s_{12}+s_{23})(1-z)=X_7+X_5-X_2\\ & W_{26} \rightarrow \frac{s_{12}z}{s_{23}(z-1)\beta} & =X_{10}\\ & W_{27} \rightarrow 1\\ & W_{28} \rightarrow 1\\ & W_{29} \rightarrow \frac{s_{12}z}{s_{23}(z-1)\beta}=X_{10}\\ & W_{30} \rightarrow \frac{s_{23}(z-1)\beta}{s_{12}z}=-X_{10}\\ & W_{31} \rightarrow (s_{12}+s_{23})(s_{12} z + s_{23}\beta(1-z))\delta & = X_{11} \end{array}$$ We recover an alphabet made of 10 multiplicatively independ letters.
